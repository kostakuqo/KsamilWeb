I became a mussel expert now too!

Ksamil is one of the main tourist destinations during the summer. We first visited it as a family when I was 8 years old. Back then, it looked beautiful, just like in the internet pictures. Today, the only beautiful sight is facing the islands. If you turn back time, you’ll see a mountain with buildings. Hotels are taking shape, open pits, or residential houses being reconstructed for rent. If you plan to visit Ksamil, do it in May or September. During the season, the beaches are packed with people, small coves overflowing with closely pitched tents. There’s no privacy for your enjoyment. You’re so close to your neighboring camper that you could accidentally steal their beer, thinking it’s yours. I found myself in Ksamil for the Mussel Festival. It’s organized annually by the Municipality of Saranda in honor of the so-called Queen of the area. “Perhaps to quell rumors that mussels have salmonella, this festival is organized,” I thought to myself. The festival was centered around the road that leads to the islands. In fact, I imagined it being somewhere by the sea or along the promenade of Ksamil. What do I need as a tourist with the streets of the neighborhood when I’ve come to see the sea? The local children were dressed in traditional clothes or animated costumes, like sea animals. For example, someone was dressed as a fish, another as a mermaid. Some even held olive branches in their hands. It felt like the times of the Greeks who held such parades. The locals seemed to be enjoying themselves until the moment the best student of Ksamil raised the flag in the center of the village. Just watching the other kids how jealous they were brought me joy. In such cases, it feels good to appreciate the effort. In my time, I was at most on the honor roll. It never occurred to anyone to respect us as distinguished students. What impressed me were the various dishes with mussels. Mussels in pie? What about mussels in kebabs? Have you ever heard of meatballs with mussels? All sorts of appetizers. I never imagined that one could cook different types of dishes with mussels. It reminded me of the movie “Two Times Fish” that says: “A fish and mussel expert.” Once I got a stomach ache from eating too many mussels. I wondered, just by looking at them, what the restaurants in the area had cooked with the main ingredient, the Queen of Ksamil. I heard that tours are organized on Lake Butrint to see up close how mussels are grown. Someone takes you there by boat and explains the process. I decided to leave that for another time. I can’t wait to one day call myself a “mussel expert” because I’m far from being an expert on fish. Article by Fjorda Llukmani
